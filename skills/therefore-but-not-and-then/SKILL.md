# Therefore / But, Not "And Then"

## Why This Exists

Trey Parker and Matt Stone gave the cleanest test for whether a story has momentum. Take your beats and read them aloud with the connective word between them. If the honest word is *and then* — "this happens, **and then** this happens, **and then** this happens" — the story is a list, and a list has no pull. If the honest word is *therefore* or *but* — "this happens, **therefore** this happens, **but** this happens" — every beat is *caused by* or *colliding with* the one before it, and the audience is carried.

This is not a storytelling gimmick. It's how consequence works. A scene where each moment earns the next has gravity. A scene that just accumulates events is something you watch from outside.

## The Rule

Between any two beats, exactly one of these should be true:

- **Therefore** — beat B happens *because* of beat A. The party freed the prisoner; **therefore** the warden now hunts them.
- **But** — beat B *complicates or reverses* beat A. The party freed the prisoner; **but** he was the one who betrayed them.

If the only honest connective is *and then* (or *also*, *next*, *meanwhile*, *the first thing… the second thing*), the beat is sequence, not story. Three options:

1. **Cut it.** If a beat doesn't cause or complicate, the scene rarely misses it.
2. **Find its causation.** Often the *therefore* is there and you hid it under a spatial transition. Surface it.
3. **Make it a "but."** Introduce the friction that turns a step into a turn.

## In Practice

**A campaign arc** is a *therefore/but* chain at the macro scale: the players' choice in session 3 **therefore** the faction's reprisal in session 5; the reprisal **but** an unexpected ally. Map the chain before the session — if two sessions connect only by "and then they traveled to the next town," the campaign has gone slack.

**A combat or scene** is the same at the micro scale. Not "the orc attacks, *and then* the rogue attacks, *and then* the wizard casts." Rather: the orc drops the fighter (**therefore** the cleric breaks formation to heal, **but** that opens the flank). Initiative order is *and then*; consequence is *therefore/but*. Narrate the consequence, summarize the sequence.

**Dialogue** turns on the same hinge. A character makes a claim; the reply should *follow from* it (therefore) or *cut against* it (but) — not just take the next conversational turn.

## What This Is Not

- Not a ban on the words "and then" in prose — it's a test for whether your *structure* is causal. You can write the sentence "and then the door opened" if the door opening is a *therefore* of what came before.
- Not an excuse to over-explain. The causation can be felt without being stated. The reader supplies the *therefore* if you've set it up; you don't always narrate it.

## The Quick Audit

Before you ship a scene or session, read its beats in order with the connective spoken aloud:

> Beat 1 — **[therefore / but / and-then]** — Beat 2 — **[therefore / but / and-then]** — Beat 3 …

Every *and then* is a flag. Cut it, cause it, or complicate it. Zero *and thens* between major beats means the scene drives instead of lists.
