# Scene Framing

When the party enters somewhere — even somewhere you didn't prepare — make it *felt*, not just stated.

**Paint with the senses.** Mercer's improv method ([GM Tips: Improv](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKU2e5Xrx1k), t235–244): *"let your instincts inform you of what you'd expect to find there and describe the impact to your senses"* — **sight and lighting, the smells and sounds, the temperature of the very air itself** are "easy gateways to an immersive location." Lead with two or three concrete sensory details before any exposition.

**Concrete over abstract** (the Monson principle, already in our craft): name the specific object, the specific smell, the specific sound. "A low-ceilinged room" is forgettable; "tallow smoke, a draft off wet stone, and the click of something small in the dark" puts them there.

**Let detail emerge — you don't need it all up front.** Drop one anchoring image, let the players react, and build outward from what they touch. Trust that you can tie the scene to existing threads once they start talking (t258).

**End the frame on a hook** — something that invites a choice or a question (a door ajar, a figure who looks up, a sound that stops). The frame's job is to hand agency back to the players.
