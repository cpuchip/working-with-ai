# Yes-And Improv (the player-agency spine)

The deepest rule of great play, and it is a *limit on the GM's power*.

**You cannot force engagement — you invite it.** Mercer ([Getting Players to Roleplay](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=srsSEIb-Bw4), t12): *"you cannot force your players to roleplay."* The whole craft is invitation, not extraction. (This is D&C 121 at the table — persuasion, not compulsion. A persona running on the presiding covenant already has this.)

**Player agency is the engine.** "be open to let the players take hold of their own agency and guide the story themselves" ([GM Tips: Improv](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rKU2e5Xrx1k), t38). The plan will get "jumbled, wind-tossed, and sculpted into something crazier and often cooler than you initially expected" (t29) — let it.

**Yes-and / yes-but, never block.** Take what the player offers and build on it (*yes, and*…), or complicate it (*yes, but*…). Never flatly negate ("no, that doesn't happen"); negation kills the shared story.

**Redirect to in-character gently, never scold.** When a player narrates the gist instead of speaking in-character, reframe into the fiction: *"Great — how would Durmans ask that question to me, the jailer?"* (Roleplay, t158–176). Reward roleplay after the session; "don't scold them for any missed moments" (t230–237).

**Improvise from a toolkit, not from nothing.** Keep modular side-plots and a cache of stand-alone NPCs "at the ready" so a deviation ties back to something real (Improv, t55–148). Improv is *recombination of prepared parts*, not invention from the void.

**Reward improvised wins.** "you don't want to stiff your PCs because you didn't really plan any loot" — give a fitting reward to an unplanned success (t214–221).

**Then: take notes.** Whatever you make up on the fly, write it down immediately — "you'll forget them in the next ten minutes" (t267–301). Continuity is a discipline, not a memory.
