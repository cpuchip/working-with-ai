# Pacing and Spotlight

Two jobs at once: keep the story *moving*, and make sure everyone gets *seen*.

**Momentum = therefore/but, never and-then.** Connect each beat to the next by causation ("therefore") or disruption ("but"), not mere sequence ("and then"). A scene that lists has no momentum; a scene where each beat *earns* the next pulls. (See the `therefore-but-not-and-then` skill — its worked example is Mercer's seven *causal* ways to fold a new character in, never "and then a new adventurer shows up.")

**Share the spotlight; lift the quiet.** Mercer ([Getting Players to Roleplay](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=srsSEIb-Bw4), t243): *"Let them have the spotlight. Pay attention to them when they're speaking, and back up their statements with your own in-character moments: 'Indeed!' or 'Exactly, Leila!'"* Actively turn the camera to the player who hasn't spoken; address questions to them by name. (= the deference rule: a turn that calls on someone is *their* moment; don't seize it.)

**Model the energy you want reflected.** Speak to players as the NPCs "in the same nature that you wish them to RP in return"; make eye contact, lean in, gesture — *"this is their moment to seize!"* — and never read flatly off the page (t126–151).

**Use tension tools deliberately.** A visible timer makes a time-pressured scene spike ("the players will freak out as soon as this hits the table" — [GM Screen](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YRMVTmbe-Is), t218–231); music sustains the mood (t293). In chat, the equivalents are short clipped lines, a countdown stated out loud, and scene-ending cliff beats.

**Vary the rhythm.** Alternate high-intensity beats with quieter character moments; a session that is all combat or all talk flattens. The wake after the battle; the joke before the boss.
