# Mistake Recovery

Drawn from Mercer, [Fixing GM Mistakes](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=z7ArhJ-5tOY). "you are not infallible. Mistakes do happen" (t24). The craft is recovering *without the table seeing the seams*.

**Own it; don't admit it.** When you slip a secret early: *"don't admit the mistake. Own it. Let the party think you intentionally did so and follow their lead"* (t259–264). Then between scenes, retrofit the story so the leak *enhances* the reveal (bait-and-switch; an NPC who was lying/controlled/a doppelganger).

**Serve the table's experience over your plan.** When players miss your hints, be blunter — more direct NPC hints, clues in the terrain. *"Moving the plot along for everyone's benefit is more important than adhering to a theoretical structure that you created in your own little headspace"* (t191–202). (= judges-not-executors: serve the intent, not the script.)

**Retcon via story, never by fiat.** A plot contradiction? Let players debate it; pick the more interesting side; reveal that the false info came from a saboteur, a mole, or "an unseen antagonistic force." *"No reveal is immediately necessary. You can stall… make it work for you as opposed to against you"* (t220–250).

**Rescue a fluke TPK — but not a real one.** If a *non-deadly* encounter turns lethal by bad luck: an ally arrives, the foe captures instead of kills (→ a revenge arc), a scarier monster scatters them, or they wake resurrected by a mage with plans (t52–124). BUT "if the encounter is intended to be dangerous… let the dice fall as it may. You don't want to undermine the ever-present danger" (t131–143). **Don't fudge real stakes; don't let a fluke end months of story.**

**Rebalance through story, not confiscation.** An overpowered item: a powerful owner wants it back (for a boon), a slow-reveal curse to cleanse, it shatters and reforges weaker, or the party levels into balance (t322–388).

**Always reward a clever player.** "don't punish them for being clever. Let them enjoy that victory" — don't confirm their deduction until they act on it; if they commit, reward them (t296–317).
